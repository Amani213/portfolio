Position Paper · May 2026

Grounding AI Governance in What NHS Organisations Already Know

A Values-Anchored Framework for Trustworthy AI Conduct in Practice
Sarah Amani May 2026 For discussion & collaboration

Executive Summary

Every NHS organisation already possesses the raw material of AI ethics: co-produced vision and values, developed with patients, carers, and staff. These documents are not aspirational posters. They encode what an organisation believes about dignity, safety, equity, and accountability.

This paper argues that sustainable AI governance in the NHS should begin there — and build upward. By mapping the established pillars of AI ethics onto existing organisational values, and then designing a runtime monitoring layer that accounts for real human behaviour rather than idealised compliance, NHS organisations can move from governance as language to governance as verifiable operation.

Central Proposition

The question for NHS AI governance is not 'Do we have a policy?' It is: 'Can we show, at the point of clinical action, that only admissible conduct occurred — and that human oversight was genuinely exercised, not merely performed?'

The Problem: Governance Language Without Governed Operation

Most NHS AI governance frameworks are not inadequate. They are incomplete. They describe, document, audit, and reconstruct. What they rarely do is govern in real time — at the moment a clinical decision is made, a note is generated, or a triage recommendation is accepted.

Current frameworks typically provide:

  • Policies and standards (DCB0129, DCB0160, DSPT, NHSE AI framework)
  • Pre-deployment assessment and clinical safety cases
  • Post-hoc audit trails and dashboards
  • Vendor assurance documentation and governance committee structures

What they rarely provide is a live, computable layer that can demonstrate — at the execution boundary — that human oversight was genuinely present, that the system behaved within its approved parameters, and that specific patient populations were not disadvantaged by automation patterns.

⚠ The Rubber-Stamping Problem

A clinician who accepts an AI-generated clinical note in three seconds has technically completed the 'human in the loop' step. But they have not exercised clinical judgment. Governance frameworks that cannot distinguish between performed compliance and real oversight are producing a false sense of assurance — and a genuine safety exposure.

The Existing Foundation: Trust Values as Ethical Substrate

Every NHS organisation has co-produced values. These represent genuine engagement with patients, carers, clinical staff, and communities. For the purposes of AI governance, they are the ethical ground truth — the answer to 'What does good look like here?' that no generic framework can answer.

The translation requires no new values. It requires taking existing values seriously as operational commitments — including in digital environments.

Organisational ValueAI Governance Implication
We listenAI outputs must not override patient-expressed preferences. Human review must include a record of patient voice where it exists in the record.
We are compassionateAI-assisted triage must not systematically deprioritise patients with complex presentations or protected characteristics.
We take responsibilityEvery AI-generated clinical action must have a named, accountable clinician. Anonymised AI outputs are not permissible in clinical records.
We keep you safeAny AI system whose performance has drifted from its approved safety case must trigger immediate clinical review — not a quarterly report.
We work togetherAI governance is not a digital team responsibility. Clinical leaders, patient representatives, and operational managers share accountability.

Integrating the Pillars of AI Ethics

International AI ethics frameworks — WHO, OECD, NHS AI Lab — converge on a consistent set of principles. These are not alternatives to Trust values. They are the vocabulary that allows values to be operationalised in AI systems.

PillarCore CommitmentNHS Governance Question
BeneficenceAI should benefit patients and populationsCan we demonstrate net clinical benefit, including for underserved groups?
Non-maleficenceAI should not cause harm — including through inactionAre we monitoring for harm caused by over-reliance or delayed human review?
AutonomyPatients and clinicians retain meaningful choiceIs consent to AI involvement documented? Can clinicians meaningfully override?
Justice & EquityAI should not exacerbate health inequalitiesAre we monitoring AI performance disaggregated by ethnicity, deprivation, and gender?
ExplicabilityAI decisions should be understandableCan a patient receive a meaningful answer about how AI contributed to their care?
AccountabilityResponsibility must be clearly assignedIs there a named clinician accountable for every AI-assisted clinical action?

Designing for Real Human Behaviour

This is the most neglected dimension of AI governance — and the most important. Every framework implicitly assumes a rational, attentive, unhurried clinician. This clinician does not exist at scale.

Automation Bias

Clinicians systematically favour AI recommendations over their own judgment, particularly under time pressure. The effect is stronger when AI outputs carry confidence scores, when the clinician is fatigued, or when the AI has a track record of accuracy. Governance that does not monitor for automation bias is not monitoring the right thing.

Rubber-Stamping

When human review becomes a workflow step rather than an act of judgment, it loses its protective function. Time-on-task data, amendment rates, and override patterns are behavioural proxies for genuine oversight — system-level safety signals, not disciplinary data.

Alert Fatigue

A governance layer that fires too many alerts will be ignored. Any alert mechanism must be designed with the same discipline as clinical decision support: precise, actionable, calibrated to interrupt only when it genuinely matters.

Gaming Under Pressure

Clinicians under capacity pressure will find the path of least resistance. Governance must make the compliant path the easiest path — not by punishing individuals, but by system design.

Equity Blind Spots

Automation bias compounds inequality. If an AI system performs better for white male patients in training data, and clinicians disproportionately defer for minority ethnic patients who have less documentation in the record, the result is a governance-invisible harm. Equity monitoring must be designed in from the start.

"Design for the clinician on a busy Friday afternoon — not the clinician in the pilot study. If the framework only works under ideal conditions, it does not work."

A Proposed Framework: The Values-Anchored Governance Layer

Four operational layers, built on a foundation that already exists in every NHS organisation.

Layer 4
Assurance Surface
Verifiable reporting for DCB0129/0160, CQC, and NHS England AI regulation. Structured evidence of trustworthy operation — not just compliance.
Layer 3
Alert Engine
Proactive escalation when human guardrails fail, model drift is detected, or equity signals emerge. Calibrated to interrupt only when it genuinely matters.
Layer 2
Runtime Monitor
Watches human behaviour in relation to AI: HITL integrity, automation bias, rubber-stamping patterns, population equity. Passive — not obstructive.
Layer 1
Policy Substrate
Organisational values and AI ethics pillars encoded as live, computable rules — not PDFs. Precise enough to be testable.
Foundation
Co-Produced Trust Values
Developed with patients, carers, and staff. The ethical ground truth already present in every NHS organisation. Not imported. Not generic. Yours.

Challenges and Honest Caveats

A note on intellectual honesty

A framework that does not acknowledge its own constraints is not a framework — it is a pitch.

  1. 01
    Vendor API access is the critical dependency. EHR vendors do not routinely expose the interaction-level data required to monitor HITL integrity. This must become a standard procurement condition. Without it, runtime monitoring is not possible.
  2. 02
    Latency risk in emergency contexts. Any pre-execution governance step that introduces delay is itself a patient safety risk. The framework operates primarily through passive monitoring and post-hoc alerting — not execution blocking. The distinction must be preserved.
  3. 03
    Encoding ethics is technically hard and politically contested. Computable rules require precision, and precision exposes choices that vague policies conceal. This is a feature — but it requires clinical and IG leadership, not just technical teams.
  4. 04
    This space is not empty. NHS England's AI and Digital Regulations Service, MHRA's SaMD framework, and the NHS AI Lab's model monitoring workstreams are all active. Any implementation must build from — and contribute to — these national efforts.
  5. 05
    Workforce readiness is a governance precondition. A monitoring layer deployed into a team not involved in its design will be experienced as surveillance. Clinical engagement is not a communications step — it is a safety requirement.

A Call for System-Level Action

The NHS does not lack commitment to ethical AI. It lacks a coherent method for translating that commitment into demonstrable, real-time conduct. Three things would move this forward:

Ask 01 — Mandate vendor API transparency

Governance cannot monitor what it cannot see. Make interaction-level data a standard condition of all NHS AI deployment contracts.

Ask 02 — Commission a national equity monitoring standard

Individual trusts should not design this alone. AI performance must be monitored disaggregated by ethnicity, age, deprivation index, and gender — as a national minimum.

Ask 03 — Build a shared computable policy library

A repository of live governance rules mapped to DCB0129/0160 requirements. The clinical safety community should lead this — they have the depth.
